# Balance Sheet Basics: A Beginner's Guide

To navigate the **Colombo Stock Exchange (CSE)** effectively, you must understand that a stock price isn't just a number—it is backed by real assets and real debts. The **Balance Sheet** is a "snapshot" in time that shows exactly what a company owns and what it owes at the end of a reporting period.

While the Income Statement shows performance over time, the Balance Sheet tells you if the company has the "structural strength" to survive a market downturn.

---

## The Golden Equation

Every Balance Sheet follows a simple, unbreakable mathematical rule:
**Assets = Liabilities + Shareholders' Equity**

### 1. Assets (What the Company Owns)
Assets are resources that have value and can produce future income.
* **Current Assets:** Cash or items that can be turned into cash within a year (e.g., Inventory, Accounts Receivable).
* **Non-Current Assets:** Long-term investments like Property, Plant, and Equipment (PPE).

### 2. Liabilities (What the Company Owes)
Liabilities are obligations the company must pay to outside parties.
* **Current Liabilities:** Debts due within a year (e.g., Short-term loans, payments to suppliers).
* **Non-Current Liabilities:** Long-term commitments like bank loans or debentures.

### 3. Shareholders' Equity (The "Residual" Value)
This is the amount of money that would be left over if the company sold all its assets and paid off all its debts. It is essentially the "Book Value" belonging to you, the investor.

---

## Practical Comparison: Strong vs. Strained Balance Sheet

Using data often seen in the CSE, here is how you distinguish between a healthy company and one facing potential liquidity risks:

| Feature | Strong Balance Sheet | Strained Balance Sheet |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Cash Reserves** | High (Covers 6+ months of costs) | Low (Barely covers monthly interest) |
| **Debt-to-Equity** | Low (Mostly funded by owners) | High (Heavy reliance on bank loans) |
| **Current Ratio** | Above 1.5 (Plenty of short-term cash) | Below 1.0 (May struggle to pay bills) |
| **Asset Quality** | Modern equipment & low bad debts | Aging machinery & high unpaid bills |
| **Summary** | The company is "Liquid." It can invest in growth or pay dividends easily. | The company is "Leveraged." Most profits go toward paying off debt interest. |

---

## Why Traders Analyze the Balance Sheet
* **Solvency:** It tells you if a company is at risk of going bankrupt. 
* **Working Capital:** Traders look at the "Current Ratio" to see if the company has enough cash to run its daily operations without taking new loans.
* **Book Value:** By comparing the "Shareholders' Equity" to the current market price, you can see if a stock is trading at a discount or a premium.

> **Note for CSE Investors:** In the Sri Lankan market, many companies in the **Manufacturing** and **Export** sectors hold significant assets in foreign currency. When the LKR fluctuates, these "Dollar-denominated" assets on the Balance Sheet can significantly increase the company's Net Asset Value (NAV), even if their local sales remain the same.
