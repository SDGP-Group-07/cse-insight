# Cash Flow Analysis: A User Guide

To navigate the **Colombo Stock Exchange (CSE)** successfully, you must remember a famous market saying: "Profit is an opinion, but cash is a fact." The **Cash Flow Statement** tracks the actual movement of money in and out of a company. It is the most honest document in a financial report because it filters out "accounting tricks."

While an Income Statement can show a profit even if no cash was received, the Cash Flow Statement tells you if the company actually has the liquidity to pay its employees, its suppliers, and—most importantly—its shareholders.

---

## The Three Pillars of Cash Flow

A company moves money in three distinct ways. Understanding these "buckets" helps you see where the company's strength is coming from.



### 1. Operating Cash Flow (OCF)
This is the "Heartbeat" of the company. It represents the cash generated by core business activities (e.g., selling tea, providing banking services).
* **Healthy Sign:** OCF should ideally be higher than Net Income, showing that profits are being converted into actual cash.

### 2. Investing Cash Flow (ICF)
This tracks money spent on the future. It includes buying new machinery, building factories, or acquiring other businesses. 
* **Healthy Sign:** A negative ICF is often good—it means the company is "re-investing" in itself to grow.

### 3. Financing Cash Flow (FCF)
This shows how the company is funded. It includes taking new loans, paying back debt, or paying out **Dividends** to you.
* **Healthy Sign:** Regular negative financing cash flow often indicates the company is successfully paying back its lenders and rewarding its owners.

## Practical Comparison: "Cash-Rich" vs. "Paper-Profit" Companies

Using real-world scenarios, here is how you spot a company that is actually healthy versus one that is struggling despite "looking" profitable:

| Feature | Cash-Rich Company | Paper-Profit Company |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Revenue Recognition** | Customer pays immediately or on time. | Sales are recorded, but customers aren't paying their bills. |
| **Dividend Safety** | Dividends are paid out of actual surplus cash. | Company borrows money from a bank just to pay a dividend. |
| **Growth Funding** | Uses its own cash to buy new assets. | Must take on high-interest debt for every new project. |
| **Inventory Management** | Stock moves fast and turns into cash quickly. | Cash is "trapped" in unsold goods sitting in a warehouse. |
| **Summary** | The business is a "Cash Cow" with high financial freedom. | The business looks good on paper but is "Cash Strapped" and risky. |

## Why Traders Analyze Cash Flow
* **Free Cash Flow (FCF):** This is the "Gold Standard." It is the cash left over after the company pays for all its operations and equipment. If FCF is high, the stock price usually follows.
* **Red Flag Detection:** If Net Income is rising but Operating Cash Flow is falling, it’s a major warning sign that the company’s "profits" might be artificial.
* **Survival:** During an economic crisis, companies with high cash reserves are the ones that survive while others go bankrupt.

> **Note for CSE Investors:** In the Sri Lankan context, many companies face "Receivable Delays," especially those dealing with the government or large infrastructure projects. A company might report a massive profit, but if their **Operating Cash Flow** is negative, it means their cash is stuck as "debt" owed by others. Always check if a company is actually collecting the money it claims to be making.
