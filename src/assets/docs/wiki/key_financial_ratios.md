# Key Financial Ratios: A Beginner's Guide

To navigate the **Colombo Stock Exchange (CSE)** effectively, you cannot rely on stock prices alone. A company with a LKR 500 share price isn't necessarily "bigger" or "better" than one at LKR 50. **Financial Ratios** are the ultimate equalizer—they allow you to compare companies of different sizes and industries on a level playing field.

Think of ratios as a "Blood Test" for a business. They reveal the underlying health, efficiency, and value of a company regardless of how much noise there is in the daily news.

---

## The Three Pillars of Ratio Analysis

Investors typically group ratios into three categories to answer three specific questions: Is it profitable? Is it safe? Is it a good price?



### 1. Profitability Ratios (The Efficiency Test)
These tell you how good the company is at turning a profit from its operations.
* **Net Profit Margin:** What percentage of total sales is actual profit? (High is better).
* **Return on Equity (ROE):** How much profit is generated for every LKR 100 invested by shareholders? This is the "Gold Standard" for measuring management's talent.

### 2. Liquidity & Solvency Ratios (The Safety Test)
These tell you if the company can pay its bills and handle its debt.
* **Current Ratio:** Compares current assets to current liabilities. A ratio of **2.0** means the company has LKR 2 in cash/assets for every LKR 1 it owes.
* **Debt-to-Equity (D/E):** Shows how much the company relies on bank loans vs. its own money.

### 3. Valuation Ratios (The "Is it Cheap?" Test)
These help you determine if the stock is a bargain or overpriced.
* **P/E Ratio (Price-to-Earnings):** How much you are paying for LKR 1 of profit.
* **P/B Ratio (Price-to-Book):** Compares market price to the company's net assets.

---

## Practical Comparison: Growth Stock vs. Value Stock

Using standard CSE profiles, here is how ratios help you identify the "Personality" of a stock:

| Metric | Growth Stock (e.g., Tech/Retail) | Value Stock (e.g., Banking/Utilities) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **P/E Ratio** | High (25x+) | **Low (5x - 8x)** |
| **Dividend Yield** | Low (Reinvests all profit) | **High (Regular cash payouts)** |
| **P/B Ratio** | High (Valued for future ideas) | **Low (Valued for physical assets)** |
| **ROE** | Usually 20%+ (High growth) | 10% - 15% (Stable & steady) |
| **Summary** | You are paying for **Future Potential**. High risk, high reward. | You are paying for **Current Assets**. Safer, income-focused. |



---

## Why Traders Use Ratios
* **Identifying Undervalued Gems:** If a company has a strong ROE but a very low P/E compared to its competitors, it might be "Hidden Value" that the market hasn't noticed yet.
* **Spotting Red Flags:** A suddenly spiking Debt-to-Equity ratio can warn you of a looming bankruptcy before it happens.
* **Benchmarking:** Ratios allow you to see if **Company A** is more efficient than **Company B**, even if they operate in different sectors.

> **Note for CSE Investors:** In Sri Lanka, the **Banking sector** typically trades at very low P/E and P/B ratios (often below 1x Book Value). Don't mistake this for a "failing" business; it is a characteristic of the local market. Conversely, **Consumer Goods** companies often have higher P/E ratios because investors are willing to pay a premium for their consistent brand power and inflation-resistant sales.
